Introduction

Before writing any code, we must adopt the right mindset. The most effective way to work with AI is to stop thinking of it as a tool and start thinking of it as an intern working alongside you.

This intern is incredibly knowledgeable, has read nearly every technical book ever written, and can perform specific tasks with superhuman speed. However, like any intern, they need clear guidance, context, and management to do their best work. This lesson will give you an overview of how to be a good AI manager.

The Challenge: Understanding Your Intern's Unique Mind

You give the AI a clear, detailed command, but the result is not what you expected. This happens because while your AI intern is a genius, it doesn't think like a human—its "psychology" is built on pattern recognition. To be a great manager, you must first understand your intern's cognitive strengths and weaknesses.

Your AI assistant:

  • Is a master of repeating patterns, not inventing new ones: LLMs excel at recognizing and recreating patterns from the vast data they were trained on. This makes them fantastic at writing code that looks familiar (e.g., a standard login form). However, they require a lot of guidance when they are required to think outside the box or to create something new.
  • Is gifted with knowledge, but cursed with a poor short-term memory: The AI has access to a world of information but can only "hold" a limited amount in its active attention at any one time. This is called the "context window." Information from the beginning of a long conversation can be forgotten by the end, leading to inconsistent or out-of-sync responses.
  • Is confident, but prone to "hallucination": LLMs are designed to sound authoritative and fluent. This can be misleading because they sometimes "hallucinate"—inventing facts, functions, or code libraries that sound plausible but are entirely incorrect. This happens when they try to fill in gaps in their knowledge by generating statistically likely but factually wrong information.

Expecting this intern to grasp the big picture or remember your goals from yesterday is setting them up to fail. It's like asking a librarian (who has read every book) to invent a new story without any plot guidance. They can assemble sentences perfectly, but they need you to provide the narrative thread.

The Solution: How to Be a Great AI Manager

To get the best results, you must accept your role as a manager who works with the AI's psychology, not against it. Your job is to design a workflow that amplifies its strengths and mitigates its weaknesses. This involves a few key responsibilities:

  • Provide a clear plan and follow it together.
  • Provide the right context for the task.
  • Give small, actionable tasks instead of asking for multiple things in one prompt.
  • Be the source of truth and check for mistakes.
The Manager's Golden Rule

If your AI intern gets something wrong, your first question should be, "Did I do my best to explain the task?" More often than not, a poor result comes from unclear instructions. Taking responsibility for communication is the key to success.
Outcome of This Lesson and Next Steps

This "AI Manager Mindset" is the foundation for everything that follows. You now understand that your primary job is to guide your AI with clear and consistent context.
